## What Does "Qualified Lead" Actually Mean?

Before discussing price, you need to understand what you are buying. Not all leads are equal, and the word "qualified" gets thrown around loosely in the industry.

### Entry-Level Leads ($0.20 - $5 per lead)

These are typically form-fill leads from broad campaigns. The prospect may have expressed general interest in a category but has not indicated urgency or specific buying intent. Think newsletter signups, ebook downloads, or "learn more" forms.

- Low cost per lead
- Low conversion rate (typically 0.5% - 2%)
- High volume potential
- Requires strong nurture sequence

### Mid-Intent Leads ($10 - $75 per lead)

These leads have taken a specific action indicating interest. They may have requested a quote, filled out a detailed contact form, or engaged with comparison content. They know what they want but have not committed to a provider yet.

- Moderate cost per lead
- Moderate conversion rate (typically 2% - 8%)
- Good for businesses with solid sales processes
- Less nurturing required

### High-Intent / Sales-Ready Leads ($50 - $500 per lead)

These are prospects at the bottom of the funnel. They have often already spoken to competitors, scheduled appointments, or requested callbacks. Some are "live transfer" leads where the prospect is on the phone waiting for your sales rep.

- High cost per lead
- High conversion rate (typically 10% - 30%)
- Best for businesses with trained closers
- Immediate sales opportunity

The key takeaway: **cost per lead is meaningless without knowing intent level**. A $500 lead that closes 25% of the time is cheaper than a $5 lead that closes 0.5% of the time.

## What Factors Affect Lead Cost?

Understanding why leads cost what they do helps you negotiate and evaluate providers.

### 1. Exclusivity

Shared leads cost 50-80% less than exclusive leads, but you are competing with 3-5 other businesses. If your sales team is fast and persistent, shared leads can still be profitable. If not, exclusivity is worth the premium.

### 2. Geographic Competition

Leads in major metros (Los Angeles, New York, Chicago) cost 2-3x more than leads in smaller markets. More competition for the same prospect drives up acquisition costs.

### 3. Lead Age

Real-time leads cost the most. Aged leads (30, 60, or 90 days old) drop in price by 50-90%. Some businesses specialize in buying aged leads cheap and nurturing them long-term.

### 4. Verification Level

Basic leads may only include a name and email. Verified leads include confirmed phone numbers, income ranges, credit scores, or qualifying questionnaire responses. Each layer of verification adds cost but improves conversion.

### 5. Delivery Speed

Leads delivered in real time via API or direct integration cost more than batch-delivered leads. Speed matters because calling within 5 minutes of a lead submitting a form increases contact rates by 900%.

## Calculating Your Break-Even Cost Per Lead

Before you buy a single lead, run this formula:

**Break-even lead cost = Average Customer Value x Close Rate**

**Example 1: Real Estate Agent**
- Average commission per transaction: $8,000
- Close rate on purchased leads: 2%
- Break-even: $8,000 x 0.02 = **$160 per lead**

Any lead under $160 is theoretically profitable. An exclusive buyer lead at $50 with a 2% close rate yields a cost per acquisition of $2,500 against an $8,000 commission — a 3.2x return.

**Example 2: Insurance Agent**
- Annual policy value (first year commission): $800
- Close rate: 10%
- Break-even: $800 x 0.10 = **$80 per lead**

**Example 3: Home Improvement Contractor**
- Average job value: $12,000
- Close rate: 15%
- Break-even: $12,000 x 0.15 = **$1,800 per lead**

Home improvement leads often cost $30-$100, leaving massive profit margins even after accounting for callbacks and no-shows.

## Red Flags: When Cheap Leads Are Too Good to Be True

If a deal seems unrealistically cheap, it probably is. Watch for:

- **No verification**: Leads with fake phone numbers or disconnected emails
- **Oversold shared leads**: Sold to 10+ buyers simultaneously
- **Aged leads sold as fresh**: 90-day-old leads marketed as "real time"
- **No replacement policy**: Bad leads are your loss
- **Mandatory long-term contracts**: Traps you before you can evaluate quality

A $0.20 lead is not a qualified lead. It is a name on a list. There is nothing wrong with cheap leads if you have the systems to nurture them, but do not expect them to convert like a $100 exclusive prospect.
